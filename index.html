URBAN DEVELOPMENT IN SUBSAHARAN AFRICA

Can we see a relationship between urbanization and inequality trends?

Photo: Nairobi, the capital of Kenya and Africa's 14th largest city with 6.54 million people (Credits: Siegfried Modola/IRIN)

We live on an increasingly urban planet. As highlighted by the UN Sustainable Development Goal 11 of Agenda 2030, cities are crucial to the future development of the world, especially to Africa. By the mid-2030s, the majority of Africans will live in urban areas. This implies a dramatic shift. In 1990, Africa had the smallest number of urban dwellers in the world compared to other regions: 197 million. Now it has 472 million urban residents, and the urban population is expected to almost double again between 2015 and 2035. By 2020, the OECD (2016) forecasts Africa to have the second highest number of urban dwellers in the world (560 million) after Asia (2348 million).

The image of a rural Africa characterized by small villages and farm land is increasingly to be replaced by tall skylines of mega cities – an Africa that is the center of global urbanization.
This takes place in a larger context of significant population changes in Africa, that will shape the global demographic landscape.

When analyzing inequality in Subsaharan Africa, Ayodele Odusola et al. (2017) Tooltip text point out that Africa will account for 82.8 per cent of the world’s net population change over the period between 2015 and 2100. Africa’s population will have increased by about 3.7 billion by the next century, from 1.19 in 2015 to 4.39 in 2100 – which stands in contrast to many other parts of the world that experience a long-term decline in population growth rates or even negative growth. Crucially, Africa’s population structure is transforming as well. Besides the largest population share, Africa also will have youngest population in the world.

As described by Ayodele Odusola et al. (2017), these demographic developments are heavily influenced by factors around health (such as declining infant mortality, fertility, prevalence of HIV/AIDS), education and other social-economic circumstances. Vice versa, the demographic developments of population growth, population structure and urbanization also influence the future development of Africa. Investments in sustainable urbanization and youth development are key to unlock the wealth of human capital that Africa represents. This year’s African Economic Outlook from the OECD Development Centre, African Development Bank, and UNDP reitterates its findings from last year: "urban areas are conducive to clusters because of the higher density of people, ideas, infrastructure and services"

The various aspects and forms of demographic transformation complicate the link between population variables and inequality. What causes urbanization?

The causes of rural-urban migration are many. There are many people migrating to the urban areas, and they are doing so because the types of activities that ought to be taking place in the countryside to keep the youth in the rural areas do not exist. The main causes of urbanization in Africa include rural-urban migration; high natural rate of increase and urban bias development strategies.

Rural-urban migration refers to the movement of people from the rural to the urban areas is the most important cause of urbanization in Africa. Added to this are the predominance of young adults, and more or less equal proportions of males and females whose desire for large families is high.
Secondly, high birth and low mortality rates Africa has the highest fertility and natural rates of increase in population in the world. Data from censuses and surveys suggest levels of total fertility mostly between six and seven children per woman. Population growth has become the highest in the world because of a sustained decline in mortality due to improved medical care and sanitation.
Thirdly, researchers find an urban bias where pro-urban development strategies adopted by countries have left urban areas more developed than the rural areas. The greater opportunities cities offered in the urban areas in terms of employment, education, health and other facilities tend to attract the youth to the urban areas.

What could we interpret from the data?

Much of the literature on the urbanization process and the pronouncements of policy-makers have both hailed growing urbanization as a sign of progress (E.g. Njoh AJ (2003) Urbanization and development in sub-Saharan Africa. Cities 20: 167–174). However, more recently, it is found that the essence of this interaction is something quite different and more complex. This is also true for Africa. Given the opportunities that cities can bring, Mingxing Chen, Hua Zhang, Weidong Liu, Wenzhong Zhang (2014) studied economic growth patterns in relation to urbanization. They found no significant correlation between urbanization speed and economic growth rate.

A vivid case in point is the country of Gabon, which has a high annual urbanization speed of 1.02%, accompanied by an annual economic growth rate of –0.63% in 1980–2011.
